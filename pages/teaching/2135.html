Project 4: ePortfolio

There are two major components to the ePortfolio: the assembled assignments and reflections, and the final reflection-in-presentation.

ePortfolio

The components of a complete ePortfolio are:

  • Projects 1, 2, and 3
  • Reflections for projects 1, 2, and 3. These should be updated to reflect any revisions done to the compositions for inclusion in the ePortfolio
  • Two additional artifacts from this semester, they may be from this class or another context.
    • A short reflection for each artifact detailing why you choose it, and how you see it fitting into the work you did in this class
  • A final reflection-in-presentation that connects and draws from the assembled ePortfolio.
  • Designed intentionally to help the audience navigate your compositions and learning.

Reflection in Presentation

Reflection allows us the opportunity to process knowledge and then apply that knowledge. Through reflection, we can come to an understanding and interpret what we have learned. This semester, on several occasions, we have used reflection in this way; in this final assignment you will return to this definition of reflection.

You have also been developing your theory of composing and what your theory of writing means in terms of its relationship to your compositions–i.e., you have been exploring whether you enact your theory of composing in your own compositions. As a result of this, you have had the opportunity to create a knowledge base of writing and its practices. In this final reflection, you will be returning to your theory to discuss several questions, including (but not limited to):

  • Define your theory of composing.
  • What was your theory of composing coming into ENC 2135 (your notecard will help with this)? How has your theory of composing evolved with each project?
  • What has contributed to your theory of composing the most?
  • What is the relationship between your theory of composing and how you create(d) knowledge?
  • How might your theory of composing be applied to other writing situations both inside and outside the classroom?

For each of these questions you will need to support your ideas with your previous compositions in this course and, through these examples, interpret what you have learned. You will create a compelling argument for whatever you decide to compose for this, supported by evidence and analysis of the work complete in class this semester.

You will choose a genre to work in–letter, email, essay, journal entry, vlog, podcast, video game, song, dance number, or any genre you may desire that is approved by the instructor–that you feel best represents your goals for your reflection and then explains why you chose that genre. In turn, you will also describe how your chosen genre affects the outcome (the final product) of your reflection.

This final reflection is an opportunity for you to demonstrate your increased knowledge in composing–the practices of composing, the key terms, and any specific skills you’ve acquired. Think of this piece as another move in the evolution of your theory of composing, and a chance for you to fully explore yourself as a composer an maker of knowledge.
